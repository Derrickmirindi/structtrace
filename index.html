Course Curriculum & Study Guide

An educational reference for structural engineering students. These are the common chapter sequences covered in Strength of Materials and Finite Element Method courses. Use them alongside the interactive beam tracer to connect theory with worked results.

Strength of Materials — common chapter sequence

1. Introduction and basic concepts

Nature of loads (tension, compression, shear, bending, torsion), equilibrium, and internal forces.

2. Simple stresses and strains

Normal stress and strain, shear stress, Hooke's law, working / yield / ultimate stresses, and factor of safety.

3. Mechanical properties of materials

Stress-strain curves, elastic and plastic behavior, toughness, hardness, and brittle vs ductile materials.

4. Axially loaded members

Bars in tension/compression, statically determinate vs indeterminate axial loading, and thermal effects.

5. Torsion of circular shafts

Torsion formula, angle of twist, power transmission, and design of shafts.

6. Shear force and bending moment in beams

Load, shear, and moment diagrams for beams; relations between load, shear, and moment.

7. Stresses in beams (flexure)

Normal bending stress, flexure formula, and shear stress distribution in beams.

8. Combined stresses and strain transformation

Plane stress, Mohr's circle, principal stresses, maximum shear stress, and stress invariants.

9. Deflection of beams

Differential equation of the elastic curve, double integration method, area-moment method, and superposition.

10. Columns and buckling

Euler buckling load, slenderness ratio, inelastic buckling, and design curves.

11. Energy methods

Strain energy, Castigliano's theorem, and applications to deflection and statically indeterminate structures.

12. Advanced / optional topics

Thick-walled cylinders, rotating disks, thermal stresses, stress concentration, and fatigue.

Exact titles vary by textbook, but nearly all strength-of-materials courses cover these blocks in some form.

Finite Element Method (FEM) — representative chapter structure

Introductory FEM texts typically begin from basic concepts and matrix algebra, then build from simple 1D elements to 2D/3D elasticity, plates and shells, dynamics, and heat transfer.

1. Overview and mathematical preliminaries

What FEM is, brief history, and typical applications; review of matrices, vectors, determinants, and linear algebra.

2. Basic concepts and spring/bar elements

Discretization, nodes, elements, degrees of freedom; spring systems as examples; the general FE solution procedure.

3. Bar and beam elements (1D structural problems)

Formulation of truss and frame elements, stiffness matrix, assembly of the global system, and boundary conditions.

4. Stationary 1D field problems and weak forms

Second-order ODEs, weighted residual / variational (Galerkin) formulation, and finite element approximation for 1D diffusion/heat or elasticity problems.

5. 2D elasticity: plane stress and plane strain

Constant strain triangle (CST), quadrilateral elements, isoparametric formulation, and interpolation functions.

6. Modeling techniques and error estimation

Mesh refinement, element quality, error indicators, convergence, and post-processing / result evaluation.

7. Plates and shells

Plate bending elements, shell kinematics, and the use and limitations of plate/shell elements in structural analysis.

8. 3D elasticity and solid elements

3D continuum elements, brick/tetrahedral elements, integration schemes, and typical solid mechanics problems.

9. Structural dynamics and vibration

Mass matrices, eigenvalue problems (normal modes), and harmonic and transient response using FEM.

10. Thermal and other field analyses

Steady-state and transient heat transfer, and multi-physics coupling in FEM.

11. Implementation aspects

Data structures, element libraries, assembly algorithms, and solution of large sparse systems.

12. Advanced / optional topics

Nonlinear problems (material and geometric), contact, large deformation, mixed formulations, and specialized applications.
